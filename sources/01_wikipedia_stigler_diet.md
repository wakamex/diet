# Stigler diet

Stigler diet

Optimization problem

The **Stigler diet** is an [optimization problem](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Optimization_problem "Optimization problem") named for [George Stigler](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/George_Stigler "George Stigler"), a 1982 Nobel laureate in economics, who posed the following problem:

> For a moderately active man weighing 154 pounds, how much of each of 77 foods should be eaten on a daily basis so that the man’s intake of nine nutrients will be at least equal to the [recommended dietary allowances](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Recommended_dietary_allowance "Recommended dietary allowance") (RDAs) suggested by the [National Research Council](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/National_Research_Council_(United_States) "National Research Council (United States)") in 1943, with the cost of the diet being minimal?

The nutrient RDAs required to be met in Stigler's experiment were [calories](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Calories "Calories"), [protein](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Protein "Protein"), [calcium](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Calcium "Calcium"), [iron](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Iron "Iron"), as well as vitamins [A](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Vitamin_A "Vitamin A"), [B1](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Thiamine "Thiamine"), [B2](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Riboflavin "Riboflavin"), [B3](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Niacin_(substance) "Niacin (substance)"), and [C](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Ascorbic_acid "Ascorbic acid"). The result was an annual budget allocated to foods such as [evaporated milk](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Evaporated_milk "Evaporated milk"), cabbage, dried navy beans, and beef liver at a cost of approximately $0.11 a day in 1939 U.S. dollars.

While the name "Stigler Diet" was applied after the experiment by outsiders, according to Stigler, "No one recommends these diets for anyone, let alone everyone." The Stigler diet has been much ridiculed for its lack of variety and palatability; however, his methodology has received praise and is considered to be some of the earliest work in [linear programming](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Linear_programming "Linear programming").

## Linear programming problem

The Stigler diet question is a [linear programming](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Linear_programming "Linear programming") problem. Lacking any sophisticated method of solving such a problem, Stigler was forced to utilize heuristic methods in order to find a solution. The diet question originally asked in what quantities a 154-pound (70kg) male would have to consume 77 different foods in order to fulfill the recommended intake of 9 different nutrients while keeping expenses at a minimum. Through "trial and error, mathematical insight and agility," Stigler was able to eliminate 62 of the foods from the original 77 (these foods were removed because they lacked nutrients in comparison to the remaining 15). From the reduced list, Stigler calculated the required amounts of each of the remaining 15 foods to arrive at a cost-minimizing solution to his question. According to Stigler's calculations, the annual cost of his solution was $39.93 in 1939 dollars (equivalent to $903in 2024, or $2 per day, slightly under the [international poverty line](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/International_poverty_line "International poverty line") as housing, clothing and fuel are also needed to live).[\[1\]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Stigler_diet#cite_note-1) The specific combination of foods and quantities is as follows:

Stigler's 1939 Diet

The 9 nutrients that Stigler's diet took into consideration and their respective recommended daily amounts were:

| Niacin | 18 milligrams |
| Ascorbic Acid (Vitamin C) | 75 milligrams |

Table of nutrients considered in Stigler's diet

Seven years after Stigler made his initial estimates, the development of [George Dantzig](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/George_Dantzig "George Dantzig")'s [simplex algorithm](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Simplex_algorithm "Simplex algorithm") made it possible to solve the problem without relying on [heuristic](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Heuristic "Heuristic") methods. The exact value was determined to be $39.69 (using the original 1939 data). Dantzig's algorithm describes a method of traversing the vertices of a [polytope](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Polytope "Polytope") of N+1 dimensions in order to find the optimal solution to a specific situation.

In 2014, [Google](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Google "Google") chef Anthony Marco devised a recipe using a similar list of ingredients (with calf liver in place of evaporated milk), called "Foie Linéaire à la Stigler"; one Google employee described it as "delicious".[\[2\]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Stigler_diet#cite_note-2)
