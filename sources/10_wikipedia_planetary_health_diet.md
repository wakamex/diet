The **planetary health diet (PHD)**, also called the **EAT-Lancet diet**, **planetary diet**, or **planetarian diet**, is a diet created by the EAT-Lancet commission[\[1\]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Planetary_health_diet#cite_note-1)[\[2\]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Planetary_health_diet#cite_note-Summary_Report-2) as part of a report released in _[The Lancet](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/The_Lancet "The Lancet")_ on 16 January 2019.[\[3\]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Planetary_health_diet#cite_note-Willett2019-3) The diet is designed to be [healthy](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Healthy_diet "Healthy diet"), adaptable to diverse cultures, and [environmentally sustainable](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Sustainable_diet "Sustainable diet") for a world population of up to 10 billion people.[\[3\]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Planetary_health_diet#cite_note-Willett2019-3)

The PHD is a [flexitarian](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Flexitarianism "Flexitarianism") diet consisting largely of plant-based foods: vegetables, fruits, whole grains, legumes, nuts, and unsaturated oils. It includes moderate amounts of fish and shellfish, poultry, dairy, and eggs.[\[3\]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Planetary_health_diet#cite_note-Willett2019-3) [Red meat](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Red_meat "Red meat") (beef, lamb, and pork) is included in small quantities.[\[3\]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Planetary_health_diet#cite_note-Willett2019-3) [Processed meat](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Processed_meat "Processed meat"), highly [processed foods](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Food_processing "Food processing"), added sugars, refined grains, and starchy vegetables are minimized.[\[3\]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Planetary_health_diet#cite_note-Willett2019-3)

Since poor diets are a major cause of health problems and death, the report estimated that universal adoption of the PHD would prevent 11 million deaths per year, largely by preventing [coronary heart disease](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Coronary_artery_disease "Coronary artery disease"), [stroke](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Stroke "Stroke"), [type-2 diabetes](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Type_2_diabetes "Type 2 diabetes"), some [cancers](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Cancer "Cancer"), and other diseases.[\[3\]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Planetary_health_diet#cite_note-Willett2019-3) In high-income countries the PHD is generally affordable and studies indicate that it does not need to cost more than typical diets for those countries.[\[4\]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Planetary_health_diet#cite_note-Hirvonen2020-4) However, it has been estimated that at least 1.58 billion people in low- and middle-income countries would not be able to afford the PHD.[\[4\]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Planetary_health_diet#cite_note-Hirvonen2020-4)

The PHD was largely well-received by the academic community.[\[5\]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Planetary_health_diet#cite_note-Tulloch2023-5) In social media discussion and in the media, responses to the report were highly polarised and the report became framed as a [culture war](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Culture_war "Culture war") issue. Evidence later emerged that a public relations firm working with the Animal Agricultural Alliance had organised a backlash against the PHD.[\[6\]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Planetary_health_diet#cite_note-Carlile20252-6)

## Definition, composition

The PHD is largely a [plant-based diet](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Plant-based_diet "Plant-based diet") consisting of vegetables, fruits, whole grains, legumes, nuts, and unsaturated oils (olive, soybean, canola, sunflower, and peanut oil). It optionally includes moderate amounts of [fish](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Fish_as_food "Fish as food") and [shellfish](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Shellfish "Shellfish"), [poultry](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Poultry "Poultry"), [dairy products](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Dairy_product "Dairy product"), and [eggs](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Eggs_as_food "Eggs as food").[\[3\]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Planetary_health_diet#cite_note-Willett2019-3)

[Red meat](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Red_meat "Red meat") (beef, lamb, and pork) can be included in small quantities, which is why it is called [flexitarian](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Flexitarianism "Flexitarianism").[\[3\]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Planetary_health_diet#cite_note-Willett2019-3) [Processed meat](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Processed_meat "Processed meat"), highly [processed foods](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Processed_foods "Processed foods"), added sugars, refined grains, and starchy vegetables such as potatoes are minimized.[\[3\]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Planetary_health_diet#cite_note-Willett2019-3)

Globally, the report calls for a more than 50% reduction in "unhealthy foods, such as red meat and sugar."[\[3\]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Planetary_health_diet#cite_note-Willett2019-3) Red meat is associated with higher risk of [cardiovascular disease](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Cardiovascular_disease "Cardiovascular disease"), [diabetes](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Diabetes "Diabetes"), and some cancers.[\[3\]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Planetary_health_diet#cite_note-Willett2019-3) Global adoption of the diet would entail more than doubling the global consumption of nuts, fruits, vegetables, and legumes.[\[3\]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Planetary_health_diet#cite_note-Willett2019-3)

A 2020 comparison study found agreements between the planetary diet and the [2015-2020 Dietary Guidelines for Americans](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Dietary_Guidelines_for_Americans "Dietary Guidelines for Americans"). The differences are in the recommended amounts of fruit, nuts, red meat, seeds, starchy vegetables and whole grains.[\[7\]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Planetary_health_diet#cite_note-7)

As of 2023 several studies have proposed variations of the PHD that are adapted to different cultures and regions. For instance, a variation of the PHD has been created for Denmark and a different one for Italy.[\[8\]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Planetary_health_diet#cite_note-8) In 2024, lower-calorie versions of the diet have also been created for those who are trying to lose weight.[\[9\]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Planetary_health_diet#cite_note-9)

## History

The EAT-Lancet Commission was an independent scientific body consisting of 19 commissioners and 18 co-authors.[\[3\]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Planetary_health_diet#cite_note-Willett2019-3) Commission members came from 16 countries, working in the fields of health, agriculture, political science, and environmental science.[\[3\]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Planetary_health_diet#cite_note-Willett2019-3)

The commission was tasked with using the best available evidence to determine a universal reference diet that is [healthy](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Healthy_diet "Healthy diet") for both humans and the planet, minimising [chronic disease](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Chronic_disease "Chronic disease") risks and maximising human wellbeing.[\[4\]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Planetary_health_diet#cite_note-Hirvonen2020-4) The diet is intended for individuals aged 2 years and above who are generally healthy, and is designed to be adaptable for all regions and culinary traditions of the world.[\[3\]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Planetary_health_diet#cite_note-Willett2019-3)

Combined with improved agricultural production practices and a 50% reduction in [food waste and loss](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Food_waste_and_loss "Food waste and loss"), the Commission estimated that this diet would provide healthy nutrition to 10 billion people, the projected population of the Earth in 2050, and be environmentally sustainable. The report assessed environmental sustainability using the [planetary boundaries](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Planetary_boundaries "Planetary boundaries") framework, considering the effects of the food system on [climate change](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Climate_change "Climate change"), [land systems](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Land_systems "Land systems") change, [freshwater](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Fresh_water "Fresh water") use, [biodiversity loss](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Biodiversity_loss "Biodiversity loss"), and [nitrogen](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Nitrogen_cycling "Nitrogen cycling") and [phosphorus](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Phosphorus_cycle "Phosphorus cycle") cycling.[\[4\]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Planetary_health_diet#cite_note-Hirvonen2020-4) The global [food system](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Food_system "Food system") is responsible for 25% to 30% of greenhouse gas emissions.[\[11\]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Planetary_health_diet#cite_note-11) Agriculture takes up 70% of global freshwater use and half of the world's habitable land surface.[\[12\]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Planetary_health_diet#cite_note-12)

## Health effects

The EAT-Lancet report estimated that if the PHD were universally adopted, 11.1 million deaths per year would be prevented.[\[3\]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Planetary_health_diet#cite_note-Willett2019-3) This would be accomplished largely by preventing [coronary heart disease](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Coronary_artery_disease "Coronary artery disease"), [stroke](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Stroke "Stroke"), [type-2 diabetes](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Type_2_diabetes "Type 2 diabetes"), some [cancers](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Cancer "Cancer"), and other diseases.[\[3\]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Planetary_health_diet#cite_note-Willett2019-3) The PHD would also reduce rates of child [stunting](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Stunted_growth "Stunted growth"), which is common in low-income countries.[\[3\]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Planetary_health_diet#cite_note-Willett2019-3)

A 2024 review of 28 studies found that the PHD was significantly associated with lower rates of diabetes, [cardiovascular disease](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Cardiovascular_disease "Cardiovascular disease"), cancer and all-cause mortality.[\[13\]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Planetary_health_diet#cite_note-Liu2024-13) Some studies found that a PHD could be deficient in certain micronutrients.[\[5\]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Planetary_health_diet#cite_note-Tulloch2023-5) Vitamin and mineral supplements are not explicitly part of the PHD, but the report indicates they can sometimes be necessary. For instance, the report recommends [iron supplements](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Iron_supplement "Iron supplement") for adolescent girls who do not eat meat.[\[3\]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Planetary_health_diet#cite_note-Willett2019-3)

A 2025 meta-analysis found that higher adherence to the PHD was associated with lower all-cause mortality and a reduced cancer risk in participants from [NHANES](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/NHANES "NHANES") and [UK Biobank](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/UK_Biobank "UK Biobank").[\[14\]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Planetary_health_diet#cite_note-14)

## Present dietary situation

Poor diets are a major cause of health problems and death, due to both inadequate consumption of nutritious foods and excess consumption of harmful ones.[\[4\]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Planetary_health_diet#cite_note-Hirvonen2020-4) Globally, approximately 800 million people are [undernourished](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Malnutrition "Malnutrition"), around 2 billion adults are overweight or obese, and over 2 billion people have micronutrient deficiencies.[\[4\]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Planetary_health_diet#cite_note-Hirvonen2020-4)

[![](https://upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/b/b0/Eat-lancet-diet-animal-products.png/1280px-Eat-lancet-diet-animal-products.png)](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/File:Eat-lancet-diet-animal-products.png) To transition to a planetary health diet, rich countries would need to reduce (but not eliminate) meat consumption. Poor countries could increase their meat consumption.[\[15\]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Planetary_health_diet#cite_note-OWID-eat-lancet-animal-15)

## Cost

[![](https://upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/9/9a/Average-per-capita-fruit-intake-vs-minimum-recommended-guidelines.png/500px-Average-per-capita-fruit-intake-vs-minimum-recommended-guidelines.png)](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/File:Average-per-capita-fruit-intake-vs-minimum-recommended-guidelines.png) The countries shown in pink consume less fruit than recommended by the PHD and other dietary guidelines. Fruit is difficult to afford for people with low incomes.[\[16\]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Planetary_health_diet#cite_note-16)

An [IFPRI](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/International_Food_Policy_Research_Institute "International Food Policy Research Institute") and Tufts University study found that based on 2011 food prices, the minimum daily cost of the PHD would range from around $2.42 in low-income countries to $2.66 in high-income countries.[\[4\]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Planetary_health_diet#cite_note-Hirvonen2020-4)

These costs are affordable for most of the world, but exceed household income for at least 1.58 billion people.[\[4\]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Planetary_health_diet#cite_note-Hirvonen2020-4) The PHD recommends larger quantities of relatively expensive food groups such as dairy, eggs, meat, fish, fruits, and vegetables in place of the high-starch foods that typically dominate the diets of the very poor.[\[4\]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Planetary_health_diet#cite_note-Hirvonen2020-4) The study also found that the PHD was about 60% more expensive than the cheapest diet that would provide adequate nutrition.[\[4\]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Planetary_health_diet#cite_note-Hirvonen2020-4)

In high-income countries, the minimum daily cost of the PHD is less than that of a typical diet for those countries.[\[5\]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Planetary_health_diet#cite_note-Tulloch2023-5) However, in high-income countries, the cost and availability of healthy food varies significantly between regions.[\[17\]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Planetary_health_diet#cite_note-Goulding2020-17) In Mexico, a study found that people who followed PHD-like diets tended to have lower food costs.[\[18\]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Planetary_health_diet#cite_note-18)

The EAT-Lancet report spurred discussions on how changes to government policies could be used to make healthy diets more affordable. In India, for instance, nearly all [food subsidies](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Food_subsidies "Food subsidies") target rice and wheat, but could be shifted or broadened to cover more nutrient-dense foods.[\[19\]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Planetary_health_diet#cite_note-19)

## Response

Social media discussion of the EAT-Lancet report was polarised.[\[20\]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Planetary_health_diet#cite_note-Garcia20192-20) Analysis of [Twitter](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Twitter "Twitter") activity indicates that opposition to the EAT-Lancet report was organized before the report's release in January 2019.[\[20\]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Planetary_health_diet#cite_note-Garcia20192-20) The online counter-movement used the hashtag #yestomeat and focused on the report's recommendation to reduce global meat consumption.[\[6\]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Planetary_health_diet#cite_note-Carlile20252-6) The #yestomeat hashtag was not used at all until January 14, 2019. At that point, which was three days before the EAT-Lancet report was released, the hashtag started to appear in hundreds of tweets each day.[\[20\]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Planetary_health_diet#cite_note-Garcia20192-20)

Evidence later emerged that the Twitter backlash against the EAT-Lancet report had been instigated by a public relations company called Red Flag on behalf of the Animal Agriculture Alliance.[\[21\]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Planetary_health_diet#cite_note-RedFlag2-21) Red Flag also encouraged academics, journalists, and the [Institute of Economic Affairs](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Institute_of_Economic_Affairs "Institute of Economic Affairs") to portray the EAT-Lancet report as "radical", "out of touch" and "hypocritical".[\[21\]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Planetary_health_diet#cite_note-RedFlag2-21) Environmental scientist [Jennifer Jacquet](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Jennifer_Jacquet "Jennifer Jacquet") commented that "Red Flag turned EAT-Lancet into a [culture war](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Culture_war "Culture war") issue."[\[6\]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Planetary_health_diet#cite_note-Carlile20252-6)

Media coverage was also divided.[\[22\]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Planetary_health_diet#cite_note-22) The UK newspaper _[The Guardian](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/The_Guardian "The Guardian")_[\[23\]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Planetary_health_diet#cite_note-23) and the US news outlet [CNN](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/CNN "CNN")[\[24\]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Planetary_health_diet#cite_note-24) have given the diet positive coverage. Harry Harris, writing in _[New Statesman](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/New_Statesman "New Statesman")_, was wary of claims that the diet could transform the world's [food system](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Food_system "Food system").[\[25\]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Planetary_health_diet#cite_note-25)

The [World Health Organization](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/World_Health_Organization "World Health Organization") withdrew its sponsorship of an EAT-Lancet event following criticism from Gian Lorenzo Cornado, Italy's [Permanent Representative to the United Nations Office at Geneva](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Permanent_Representative_to_the_United_Nations_Office_at_Geneva "Permanent Representative to the United Nations Office at Geneva").[\[26\]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Planetary_health_diet#cite_note-:1-26)[\[27\]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Planetary_health_diet#cite_note-27) Cornado said that adopting one dietary approach for the whole planet would destroy traditional diets and cultural heritage, and that reducing meat and candy consumption would cause the loss of millions of jobs.[\[26\]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Planetary_health_diet#cite_note-:1-26)

Sixteen cities from the [C40 Cities Climate Leadership Group](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/C40_Cities_Climate_Leadership_Group "C40 Cities Climate Leadership Group"), including New York, Seoul, and Milan, have committed to using the PHD to guide municipal food policies.[\[28\]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Planetary_health_diet#cite_note-28)

Academic reception of the 2019 EAT-Lancet report was generally positive.[\[5\]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Planetary_health_diet#cite_note-Tulloch2023-5) Studies praised the report for providing quantified science-based recommendations that could guide the development of food policies.[\[5\]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Planetary_health_diet#cite_note-Tulloch2023-5) The most common criticism was the report's lack of attention to socioeconomic issues such as cultural food preferences and agricultural jobs.[\[5\]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Planetary_health_diet#cite_note-Tulloch2023-5)
